Overview

Hinge entered a period of rapid growth in 2020,
from 60 FTEs to 200+ in 2021.

In onboarding, I observed our CEO, Justin, was pretty in the weeds with some smaller projects, and it acted as a blocker for the team and distractor for him. This presented an unscalable bottleneck without a clear way to push decision making out to the front lines.

Institutionalized process was relatively new to Hinge. We had our work cut out for us.

Objective

Open the CEO's bandwidth for bigger, long-term initiatives.

Strategic

Open the CEO's bandwidth for bigger, long-term initiatives.

Functional

Implement a scalable decision making model that provides the team with a universal language to assign roles for XFN projects including one, clear decision maker for key gates.

Hinge HQ · New York

Define, Plan, Execute

Making a decision about decision making.

01
Define: Understand the problem

I focused on two popular decision making frameworks when bringing a recommendation to Hinge C-Suite. RACI, the more popular model, solves for general lack of role clarity in external or internal programs. RAPID, designed by Bain, provides a framework for roles that can be applied to projects, and forces one clear decision maker at key gates. There can only be one decision maker, and that is why we moved forward with RAPID.

02
Plan: Map the work and milestones

Identify our baseline. Develop an operating model for engagement with leadership. Establish our working team and pilot project. Build milestones alongside scalable solutions for H2 2021 and beyond.

03
Execute: Comms, tools, staying organized

XFN team syncs for the pilot project: one to keep stakeholders informed, one for the working team. 1:1s to discuss blockers, open questions, unclear scenarios. Documentation on RAPID was built into the Notion company intranet and launched at All Hands rollout. This content was used to build an onboarding session on RAPID for all new hires.

Were we successful?

Prior to RAPID, 63% of FTEs reported feeling unclear about roles and decision makers in XFN projects.

This was 48% at 3 months post rollout, and 32% at 6 months. Pushing decision making to the front lines meant our CEO could shift focus to broader initiatives like social impact and global market strategy.

Recruiting leaders and culture carriers to champion the change and illustrating the benefits of institutionalized framework through a pilot project meant adoption was effective and positive, even though big change takes time.

Challenges & learnings.

Change is hard

Institutionalizing process in a culture that prided itself on doing things differently meant investment in understanding our needs to translate use case to serve the org and compel teams to adopt this change long term.

Coach up, do it again

Coaching our C-suite was a continuous process from initiation through implementation to make sure everyone was brought along for the journey.

Recruit your champions

The key to success was to identify culture carriers to champion this change, provide feedback and channel the value of this framework up, down, and to XFN peers through consistent practice. Even so, this type of change takes time.
